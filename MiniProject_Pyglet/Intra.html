Project - pyglet








Intro








Your mission




The goal of this project is to create a small 2D video game "world". 
The game won't really be playable, but you will create a world that 
scrolls in the window. To do that, you will use the pyglet library (https://pyglet.readthedocs.io). Don't worry, we will do it step by step, together.
We
 provide you with some examples, but we encourage you to try and do 
something new, something cool! Be as creative as you want (you can do a 
Mario-inspired world like us, a pirates world, a post-apo world, a 
hacker world, etc.). Your creative efforts will be rewarded during the 
staff review.
All the code for the project must be in the same 
directory. Feel free to split your code in different files (it's 
probably a good thing if you do...!). As always, commit your work and 
push as often as possible. You will probably break some of your code 
from time to time - and you don't want to have nothing to show during 
the staff review, right?
There are enough features in this project
 to split the workload easily between teammates. However don't forget 
that you are a team: you should help each other out, and you should 
understand perfectly both your work and your teammates'.
Of 
course, you don't have to code everything up to the last steps. Go as 
far as you can, experiment around (it's easy, it's graphic!), and have 
fun!

Team Agreement









Team Agreement








Role of the team agreement




You already wrote one last weekend, so you know what a team agreement
 is. Just in case, let's remind you of the main point of having a team 
agreement.
Writing a team agreement at the beginning of your group
 project clarifies expectations so that everybody knows what's their 
role and what part they should play in the project. Since everybody 
writes the team agreement document together, it's easier to enforce its 
content: everybody already agreed to follow the rules, so it's less 
awkward to remind your teammates of what they should do if any conflict 
arises.
Note: The agreement won't be graded, but it will be
 referred to on Sunday during the code review with a mentor. It's a work
 document, do it seriously.












The process




Same as last week. Meet up with your teammates, read carefully the 
subject for the project and, before starting to code anything, create a 
file called team_agreement.md with the agreed on rules. Once it's finalised, push it!












What goes in it this time?




Mostly, same as last week. However as this project has a lot of 
possible bonuses, your team agreement will also address your wishlist in
 terms of bonus features. Not everybody has the same goals, some may 
want to push the project to its limits, while others are happy just 
perfecting the code of the core features. You need to clarify your 
expectations.
The team agreement should address the following points:
    

  • the name of the team
    • 

  • the working hours each member commits to
    • 

  • how often you will check-in with each others' code
    • 

  • the strengths and weaknesses of each member
    • 

  • the list of features you wish to implement
    • 

  • the allocation of the workload
    • 

  • your strategy so that you all understand the codebase
    •

Step 01









Step 01








Add some sprites




Now that you understand how our code works, you can add some images 
(called sprites) in the window. The sprites should scroll from right to 
left, as if we were moving to the right. The sprites should appear 
randomly (like everything in your game), they should not move too fast, 
and they should be a part of the background (like clouds, stars, planes,
 trees, etc.)
In our example, we added clouds.
Be careful, 
when a sprite is no longer viewable in the window, there's no use to 
keep a reference to it. Think of your computer memory!

Step 06 (and 07 (and 08 (and ...)))




Now, you can be as creative as you want! We will give you some 
examples of features, you can add them in the order you want. Each new 
feature should follow the law of physics: nothing can go through a wall 
(except a ghost maybe?), people cannot float (but maybe ghosts and birds
 can?), etc.
Here are the features you can play with:
    

  • Non-static objects (horizontal movement, vertical movement, complex patterns, random patterns...)
    • 

  • Animated objects (it's not enough to move, animate them !)
    • 

  • Background music
    • 

  • Action music (make some noise when an object does something)
    • 

  • Pause/Unpause (by pressing P on the keyboard)
    • 

  • Click (and destroy the thing you clicked on)
    • 

  • Fire (add objects that can fire at other objects)
    • 

  • Kills (your bombshells can destroy the floor or other objects)
    • 

Note: If you play with sounds and/or music, you will have to add that line to your program:
pyglet.options['audio'] = ('directsound', 'openal', 'pulse')

This is because Pulse is unstable in your version of Ubuntu, and might cause crashes.
